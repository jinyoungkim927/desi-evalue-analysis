1. The Big Picture: What We're Measuring

The Core Question: Is dark energy a cosmological constant (fixed forever), or is it evolving over cosmic time?

To answer this, we need a cosmic ruler—something of known physical size that we can observe at different distances (and thus different times in cosmic history). By measuring how large this ruler appears at different distances, we can map the expansion history of the universe.

The ruler we use is the sound horizon: a characteristic scale imprinted in the distribution of matter when the universe was only 380,000 years old. This is the physics of Baryon Acoustic Oscillations (BAO).

The Logical Chain

  1. Early universe physics → predicts sound horizon size (~147 Mpc)
  2. Galaxy surveys → measure apparent size of this ruler at different redshifts
  3. Apparent vs. true size → tells us the distance to each redshift
  4. Distance vs. redshift relation → reveals the expansion history \(H(z)\)
  5. Expansion history → constrains dark energy properties

2. The Early Universe: Setting the Stage

The Hot Big Bang

Immediately after the Big Bang, the universe was:

  • Extremely hot: \(T > 10^{10}\) K
  • Dense: All matter compressed into tiny volume
  • Ionized: No neutral atoms; free electrons and protons
  • Opaque: Photons constantly scattered off free electrons (Thomson scattering)
Key Insight: In this hot plasma, photons and baryons (protons + neutrons) were tightly coupled. They behaved as a single fluid—the photon-baryon fluid.

The Players

ComponentBehaviorRole in BAO
Dark Matter Only feels gravity; clumps freely Creates initial overdensities (gravity wells)
Baryons Coupled to photons via electrons Dragged along by photon pressure waves
Photons Provide pressure; scatter off electrons Drive acoustic waves outward
Electrons Mediate photon-baryon coupling Enable Thomson scattering

Timeline of the Early Universe

\(t \approx 10^{-36}\) s: Inflation ends
Quantum fluctuations become density perturbations (seeds of structure)
\(t \approx 1\) s: Nucleosynthesis
Protons and neutrons form helium nuclei; establishes baryon-to-photon ratio
\(t \approx 50,000\) years: Matter-radiation equality
Matter density exceeds radiation density; dark matter starts clumping
\(t \approx 380,000\) years: Recombination
Electrons combine with protons → neutral hydrogen; photons decouple → CMB

3. Baryon Acoustic Oscillations: The Physics

Step 1: Initial Overdensity

Inflation creates tiny density fluctuations: some regions have slightly more matter than average. Consider one such overdense region:

\[\delta(\vec{x}) = \frac{\rho(\vec{x}) - \bar{\rho}}{\bar{\rho}} \sim 10^{-5}\]

This overdensity contains dark matter, baryons, and photons. The dark matter sits at the center, feeling only gravity. But the photon-baryon fluid feels both gravity and pressure.

Step 2: Pressure Wave Propagation

The Key Physics: Gravity pulls the photon-baryon fluid inward, but photon pressure pushes it outward. This creates a spherical pressure wave propagating away from the overdensity.

The wave equation for the photon-baryon fluid is approximately:

\[\ddot{\delta}_b + \frac{\dot{a}}{a}\dot{\delta}_b = c_s^2 \nabla^2 \delta_b + \frac{3}{2}\frac{\dot{a}^2}{a^2}\Omega_m \delta_{dm}\]

where the sound speed is:

\[c_s = \frac{c}{\sqrt{3(1 + R)}}, \quad R = \frac{3\rho_b}{4\rho_\gamma}\]

Early on, \(R \ll 1\), so \(c_s \approx c/\sqrt{3} \approx 0.58c\). The wave travels at about 58% the speed of light!

Step 3: Freeze-Out at Recombination

At recombination (\(z \approx 1100\), \(T \approx 3000\) K):

  1. Electrons combine with protons → neutral hydrogen
  2. Photons no longer scatter; they stream freely (become the CMB)
  3. Without photon pressure, the baryon wave freezes

The distance the wave traveled defines the sound horizon:

\[r_s = \int_0^{t_{rec}} \frac{c_s(t)}{a(t)} dt = \int_{z_{rec}}^{\infty} \frac{c_s(z)}{H(z)} dz\]

The Final Configuration

After recombination, we're left with:

  • Dark matter overdensity at the original position (center)
  • Baryon overdensity in a spherical shell at radius \(r_s \approx 147\) Mpc
Result: There's a slight preference for pairs of galaxies to be separated by ~147 Mpc (comoving). This appears as a bump in the correlation function at this scale.

Why 147 Mpc?

The sound horizon depends on early universe physics:

\[r_s \propto \frac{c_s \cdot t_{rec}}{a_{rec}} \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{\Omega_m h^2}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{\Omega_b h^2}}\]

With Planck-measured values (\(\Omega_m h^2 = 0.143\), \(\Omega_b h^2 = 0.0224\)):

\[r_s = 147.05 \pm 0.26 \text{ Mpc}\]

This is known to 0.2% precision from CMB physics!

The Galaxy Correlation Function

The two-point correlation function \(\xi(r)\) measures the excess probability of finding galaxy pairs at separation \(r\):

\[dP = n^2 [1 + \xi(r)] dV_1 dV_2\]

The BAO appears as a bump at \(r = r_s\):

What DESI Measures: The position of this bump in the correlation function at different redshifts. Comparing the observed angular/redshift separations to the known physical scale \(r_s\) gives us the angular diameter distance and Hubble parameter.

4. Dark Energy: The Mystery

The Discovery (1998)

Type Ia supernovae observations by Riess et al. and Perlmutter et al. revealed that the universe's expansion is accelerating. This requires a component with negative pressure—dark energy.

The Equation of State

Any cosmic component has an equation of state relating pressure to density:

\[P = w \rho c^2\]
Component\(w\)Density evolution
Matter (dust)0\(\rho \propto a^{-3}\)
Radiation1/3\(\rho \propto a^{-4}\)
Cosmological constant-1\(\rho = \text{const}\)
Generic dark energy\(w(a)\)Depends on \(w(a)\)

For acceleration, we need \(w < -1/3\). The cosmological constant has \(w = -1\) exactly.

The Cosmological Constant Problem

The "Worst Prediction in Physics": Quantum field theory predicts the vacuum energy density should be: \[\rho_{vac} \sim \frac{M_{Pl}^4}{c^5 \hbar^3} \sim 10^{76} \text{ GeV}^4\] But the observed dark energy density is: \[\rho_{DE} \sim 10^{-47} \text{ GeV}^4\] A discrepancy of 123 orders of magnitude!

This fine-tuning problem motivates considering alternatives where dark energy evolves rather than being a pure cosmological constant.

The CPL Parameterization

Chevallier, Polarski, and Linder proposed a simple parameterization for evolving dark energy:

\[w(a) = w_0 + w_a(1-a)\]

Or in terms of redshift:

\[w(z) = w_0 + w_a \frac{z}{1+z}\]

Where:

  • \(w_0\): equation of state today (\(a = 1\))
  • \(w_a\): rate of change (approximately \(-dw/da\) at \(a=1\))

Physical Interpretation

Model\(w_0\)\(w_a\)Physical Meaning
ΛCDM-10Cosmological constant; vacuum energy
Quintessence\(> -1\)variesSlowly-rolling scalar field
Phantom\(< -1\)variesExotic field; \(\rho\) increases with expansion
Thawing\(\approx -1\)\(< 0\)Field starts frozen, now evolving
Freezing\(> -1\)\(> 0\)Field was evolving, now slowing
DESI's Claim: Their data prefer \(w_0 \approx -0.7\) and \(w_a \approx -1.0\), suggesting dark energy was more "cosmological-constant-like" in the past (\(w \to -1\) as \(z \to \infty\)) but is becoming less negative today.

How Dark Energy Affects Distances

The Friedmann equation with dark energy:

\[H^2(z) = H_0^2 \left[\Omega_m(1+z)^3 + \Omega_{DE}(z)\right]\]

For the CPL parameterization:

\[\Omega_{DE}(z) = \Omega_{DE,0} \cdot (1+z)^{3(1+w_0+w_a)} \cdot \exp\left(-3w_a\frac{z}{1+z}\right)\]

The comoving distance is:

\[D_C(z) = c\int_0^z \frac{dz'}{H(z')}\]

Different \(w_0, w_a\) → different \(H(z)\) → different \(D_C(z)\) → different apparent BAO scale!

5. The Observational Chain

From Photons to Cosmology

Step 1: Spectroscopy

DESI's 5000 robotic fiber positioners capture galaxy spectra. From the spectrum:

  • Redshift \(z\): From emission/absorption line positions
  • Galaxy type: From spectral features

Step 2: 3D Positions

Angular position (RA, Dec) + redshift → 3D comoving position (assuming a fiducial cosmology)

Step 3: Correlation Function

Count galaxy pairs as a function of separation:

  • \(s_\perp\): Transverse separation (from angular separation)
  • \(s_\parallel\): Line-of-sight separation (from redshift difference)

Step 4: BAO Fitting

The BAO signal appears as a ring in the 2D correlation function \(\xi(s_\perp, s_\parallel)\). Fitting gives:

  • \(\alpha_\perp = D_M(z)/D_M^{fid}(z)\): Transverse dilation
  • \(\alpha_\parallel = D_H(z)/D_H^{fid}(z)\): Radial dilation

Step 5: Distance Measurements

\[\frac{D_M(z)}{r_s} = \alpha_\perp \cdot \frac{D_M^{fid}(z)}{r_s^{fid}}\]
\[\frac{D_H(z)}{r_s} = \alpha_\parallel \cdot \frac{D_H^{fid}(z)}{r_s^{fid}}\]

Step 6: Cosmological Constraints

Compare measured \(D_M(z)/r_s\) and \(D_H(z)/r_s\) to model predictions. Find best-fit \(\Omega_m\), \(w_0\), \(w_a\), etc.

Why Both \(D_M\) and \(D_H\)?

The BAO feature is observed in 2D (angular and redshift directions):

  • \(D_M\): Measures distances perpendicular to line of sight (angular diameter distance)
  • \(D_H = c/H(z)\): Measures distances along line of sight (related to expansion rate)

Having both breaks degeneracies and provides stronger constraints than either alone.

6. Why This Matters

Fundamental Physics Implications

If \(w = -1\) (Cosmological Constant):

  • Vacuum energy is real but inexplicably small
  • Requires extreme fine-tuning or anthropic selection
  • Universe eventually becomes de Sitter space; eternal exponential expansion

If \(w \neq -1\) (Evolving Dark Energy):

  • New physics beyond standard cosmology
  • Likely involves new scalar field(s)
  • Could connect to fundamental physics (string theory, modified gravity)
  • Future of universe depends on how \(w\) evolves

The Stakes of DESI's Claim

If True: Evolving dark energy would be one of the most significant discoveries in modern physics, pointing to new fundamental fields or modifications of gravity.
The Caution: Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence. The statistical significance (3-4σ) must be robust to:
  • Statistical methodology (p-values vs. e-values vs. Bayesian evidence)
  • Systematic errors in the data
  • Model selection effects (w₀wₐCDM has 2 extra parameters)
  • Tension with other datasets (CMB, supernovae)

The Role of E-Values

This is where our e-value analysis becomes crucial. By using proper validation techniques (data splitting, uniform mixture e-values), we can assess whether the apparent evidence for evolving dark energy is:

  1. A genuine signal in the data
  2. An artifact of fitting a flexible model to noise
  3. A consequence of tensions between datasets that w₀wₐCDM happens to resolve

Our finding that e-values drop from 392 → 1.4 under proper validation suggests caution is warranted.
